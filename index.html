History of BC Wine

Prior to 1990, apart from a few industry pioneers, most of the grapes grown in BC were hybrid, and the growers'focus was on quantity rather than quality. That all changed with the signing of North America Free Trade Agreement between Canada and United States. With the onset of free trade, the goverment gave the option for growers to remove their vines and plant a different crop. Two-thirds of the 3,400 grapes acreage was removed leaving just 1,134 acres of grapes, only half of which was estimated to vinifera grapes.
This launched the subsequent planting of quality vinifera varieties which saw the begining of the modern BC wine industry and the impressive BC wine we enjoy today.
Now with 30 years of experience testing different theories and determining the right place for the right grape, the BC wine industry is coming into its own and creating its own style and personality.
That combined with ambition and vine age, comes with wine that continues to gain complexity and the exceptional people who craft them.

Features of BC Wine

There are five main wine growing regions: Okanagan Valley, Vancouver Island, Fraser Valley, Similkameen Valley and the Gulf Island. Located about a four-hour drive from vancouver in south central British Columbia. Okanagan Vally features more than 100 wineries. The flavor is different depenpending of these regions.

White

Under that skin, the pulpy part of a white grape is the same color as that of a red grape. The skin dictates the end color for red wine, which differs from the white's color determinates.

Red

There are countless numbers of red grape varieties in the world, some able to make wine, others suites for the Welch's factory. The wine market focuses on about 40 - 50 different grape.

Rose

Technically, a rose is an "unfinished red wine", Rose is defferent sort of wine, with all the refreshing qualities of white wine mixed some characteristics of a red.
